Overview

The Elephanta Caves are a network of sculpted caves located on Elephanta Island, situated 11 kilometers to the east of the city of Mumbai. Known for their ancient rock-cut temples and intricate carvings, the caves are a major tourist attraction and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Location

Elephanta Island, Mumbai Harbour, Mumbai, Maharashtra, India

History

The Elephanta Caves date back to between the 5th and 8th centuries AD. They are believed to have been created during the reign of the Kalachuri dynasty and later expanded by the Chalukyas. The caves are known for their Hindu and Buddhist sculptures, including the famous Trimurti, a 20-foot depiction of the Hindu god Shiva.

Architecture

The Elephanta Caves are renowned for their intricate rock-cut temples and sculptures. The main cave, known as the Great Cave, features impressive carvings of Hindu deities, with the most notable being the Trimurti, a colossal representation of the three-headed Shiva. The caves exhibit a blend of Hindu and Buddhist architectural styles.

Visitor Information

To reach Elephanta Caves, visitors take a ferry from the Gateway of India. The island is open to tourists from 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM, and guided tours are available. It is advisable to wear comfortable walking shoes, as the caves are accessed via a steep climb and uneven paths. Carrying water and sunscreen is also recommended.
